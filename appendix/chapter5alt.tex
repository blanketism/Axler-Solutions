\subsection{Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors}

\begin{appendixexercise}[5a42]
    Define $T \in \lin{\f^n}$ by $T(x_1, x_2, x_3, \ldots, x_n) = (x_1, 2x_2, 3x_3, \ldots, nx_n)$.
    \begin{subexercises}
        \item Find all eigenvalues and eigenvectors of $T$.
        \item Find all subspaces of $\f^n$ that are invariant under $T$.
    \end{subexercises}
\end{appendixexercise}

\begin{proof}[Civil Service Pigeon]
    This proof is a generalization to any diagonalizable operator as well as not having to deal with Lagrange interpolation by hand.

    Let $U \subseteq \mathbb{F}^n$ be invariant under $T$. Let $u=\sum^{n}_{i=1} \alpha_i e_i \in U$ and suppose $\alpha_k \neq 0$ for some $k$. Since $Te_i=ie_i$, we have distinct eigenvalues $1$, $2$, $\dots$, $n$. There exists a unique polynomial $p \in \mathcal{P}(\mathbb{F})$ such that $p(i)=\delta_{i,k}$ for $k=1$, $\dots$, $n$. 

    Since $U$ is invariant under $T$, it is also invariant under $p(T)$ by 5A41:
    $$p(T)u=\sum^{n}_{i=1} \alpha_i p(T)e_i=\alpha_k e_k.$$

    Thus, $e_k \in U$ whenever $u \in U$ has a nonzero $k$th coordinate. By applying this to every index with nonzero coefficient in $u$, we see that $e_i \in U$ for every $i$ such that some element in $U$ has nonzero $i$th coordinate. Hence, $U$ is the span of $\{e_i: i \in S\}$ for some $S \subseteq \{1,\dots,n\}$. 

    Conversely, any such span is invariant under $T$ since $Te_i=ie_i$. Therefore, the invariant subspaces of $T$ are precisely the spans of subsets of $\{e_1, \dots, e_n\}$.
\end{proof}
